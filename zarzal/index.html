The Rivet and the Songbird

A Story of Heritage, Tragedy, and Memory

Welcome to the Ford T City Museum in Tacuarembó, Uruguay. A sanctuary where history is preserved not just in objects, but in the stories they whisper. Under the stewardship of Eduardo Custodio, this unique collection bridges the gap between the mechanical marvels of the early 20th century and the eternal soul of the Tango.

A Journey on Wheels

Walking through the halls of the Ford T City Hotel is stepping into a living timeline. The museum houses an impeccable collection of Ford Model T cars, the vehicle that put the world on wheels. Painstakingly restored by Eduardo Custodio, these machines shine with the same luster as they did a century ago, representing an era of innovation that coincided with the rise of a musical legend.

The Songbird's Silence

Alongside the mechanical roar of the Fords lies a quiet corner dedicated to Carlos Gardel. The museum holds a complete collection of his original discography—shellac records that captured the voice of the "Zorzal Criollo."

But the centerpiece of this homage is tragic. On June 24, 1935, at the Olaya Herrera aerodrome in Medellín, a Ford Trimotor aircraft crashed, taking Gardel from the world. For decades, the details were shrouded in sorrow and mystery.

The Witness in Metal

Resting in a display case is an object of profound historical weight: a cast aluminum fragment and a rivet recovered from the wreckage in Medellín. This is not merely scrap metal; it is a survivor of history.

Through the dedicated research of engineer and historian Mauricio Umaña, and in collaboration with the University of the Republic, this piece has been subjected to rigorous metallurgical analysis. The studies certify its composition, its age, and its provenance, confirming it as a genuine part of the fuselage that carried the idol on his final flight.
